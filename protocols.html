Guide 01

Reconstitution Protocol

Lyophilized (freeze-dried) compounds must be reconstituted with bacteriostatic water before use in research. Proper technique preserves compound integrity and prevents contamination.

  1. Allow the lyophilized vial and bacteriostatic water to reach room temperature (15–25°C). Do not heat.
  2. Swab both vial stoppers with a sterile alcohol prep pad. Allow to air dry completely before proceeding.
  3. Using a sterile syringe, draw the desired volume of bacteriostatic water. Standard reconstitution uses 1ml BAC water per vial unless otherwise specified.
  4. Insert the needle through the stopper at an angle. Inject the water slowly down the inside wall of the vial — never spray directly onto the lyophilized cake.
  5. Allow the compound to dissolve naturally. Gently swirl if needed. Do not shake, vortex, or invert repeatedly — this can denature peptide bonds.
  6. Once fully dissolved, the solution should be clear. Any cloudiness, particulate matter, or discoloration indicates potential contamination — discard the vial.
Dilution calculator: To determine concentration, divide the total compound mass (mg) by the volume of BAC water added (ml). For example: 10mg reconstituted with 2ml BAC water = 5mg/ml (or 5,000mcg/ml).

Guide 02

Storage & Stability

Proper storage is the single biggest factor in maintaining compound potency over time. Temperature, light, and contamination are the three primary degradation vectors.

  1. Pre-reconstitution (lyophilized): Store at -20°C (freezer) for long-term stability. Room temperature is acceptable for short periods during transit. Avoid repeated freeze-thaw cycles.
  2. Post-reconstitution: Refrigerate at 2–8°C (standard refrigerator). Most reconstituted compounds maintain stability for 21–30 days when stored properly.
  3. Light protection: Store vials away from direct light. UV exposure accelerates oxidation in most peptide compounds. Keep in original packaging or wrap in foil.
  4. Contamination prevention: Always use a fresh sterile needle for each withdrawal. Never touch the stopper with ungloved hands. Swab with alcohol before each use.
  5. Shelf life reference: Lyophilized (sealed, -20°C): 24+ months. Reconstituted (2–8°C): 21–30 days. Reconstituted (room temp): not recommended beyond 48 hours.
Pro tip: If you reconstitute more than you'll use within 30 days, consider splitting into smaller aliquots using sterile vials. This minimizes repeated needle punctures through the main stopper and reduces contamination risk.

Guide 03

Reading a Certificate of Analysis

A certificate of analysis (COA) is the single most important quality document for any research compound. If a vendor can't provide one, that's your first red flag.

  1. HPLC Purity: High-Performance Liquid Chromatography measures the percentage of the target compound versus impurities. Look for ≥98% minimum; >99% is the gold standard. The chromatogram should show one dominant peak.
  2. Mass Spectrometry (MS): Confirms molecular identity by measuring the compound's mass-to-charge ratio. The observed molecular weight should match the expected value within ±1 Da (dalton).
  3. Appearance: Should match expected form — most lyophilized peptides are a white to off-white powder. Yellow, brown, or wet cakes suggest degradation or manufacturing issues.
  4. Endotoxin levels: Measured in EU/mg (endotoxin units per milligram). Research-grade compounds should have <5 EU/mg. Higher levels indicate bacterial contamination during manufacturing.
  5. Batch/lot number: Every COA should reference a specific batch. If the COA is generic (no lot number), it may not correspond to your actual product — a common tactic among low-quality vendors.
Red flags: No lot number on the COA. Purity below 95%. No mass spec data. COA from a different company than the vendor. COA date more than 12 months old. Any of these should give you pause.

Guide 04

Lab Documentation Standards

Maintaining proper research records isn't just good practice — it's what separates reproducible science from guesswork. Every variable matters.

  1. Research log entries: Record the compound name, lot number, reconstitution date, volume of BAC water used, calculated concentration, and storage location for every vial.
  2. Batch tracking: Keep COAs on file organized by lot number. If results vary between batches, the COA comparison is your diagnostic starting point.
  3. Environmental conditions: Note ambient temperature, humidity, and any deviations from protocol during handling. These variables affect compound stability.
  4. Usage log: Track the date, time, and volume of each withdrawal from a reconstituted vial. This helps identify contamination timelines if issues arise.
  5. Disposal: Expired or compromised compounds should be disposed of according to local regulations. Do not pour down drains. Most jurisdictions accept pharmaceutical waste at designated collection points.
